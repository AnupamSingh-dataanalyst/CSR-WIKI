# Robin Hood tax
The Robin Hood tax, also known as the Financial Transaction Tax (FTT), is a concept that proposes a small tax on financial transactions, such as buying and selling of stocks, bonds, and derivatives. The idea behind this tax is to generate revenue for social welfare programs and to reduce speculation in financial markets. In the context of India, the Robin Hood tax is not a specific tax law, but rather a concept that has been discussed and debated in the context of financial sector taxation.

## Key Details
The Robin Hood tax is typically proposed as a small percentage of the transaction value, ranging from 0.01% to 0.1%. In India, the Securities Transaction Tax (STT) is a similar tax that is levied on transactions in securities, such as stocks, derivatives, and mutual funds. The STT rates range from 0.025% to 0.1% of the transaction value, depending on the type of security and the type of transaction. The tax is collected by the stock exchanges and deposited into the government treasury.

## Who Needs This
The Robin Hood tax concept applies to financial institutions, such as banks, brokerages, and investment firms, that engage in financial transactions. In India, companies that trade in securities, such as stocks, derivatives, and mutual funds, are required to pay the STT. Non-governmental organizations (NGOs) that invest in securities may also be subject to the STT. Additionally, companies that engage in high-frequency trading or other forms of speculative trading may be subject to the Robin Hood tax, if it were to be implemented.

## Step-by-Step Process
While the Robin Hood tax is not a specific tax law in India, companies that trade in securities are required to follow these steps to comply with the STT:
* Register with the stock exchange and obtain a unique identification number
* File returns with the stock exchange and the Income Tax Department
* Pay the STT on transactions in securities
* Maintain records of transactions and tax payments

## Important Deadlines / Penalties
The deadlines for paying the STT and filing returns vary depending on the type of security and the type of transaction. Generally, the STT is required to be paid on the date of the transaction, and returns are required to be filed on a quarterly or annual basis. Failure to pay the STT or file returns can result in penalties, such as fines and interest on the tax due.
