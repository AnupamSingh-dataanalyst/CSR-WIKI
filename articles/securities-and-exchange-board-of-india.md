# Securities and Exchange Board of India
The Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI) is the primary regulator of the securities market in India, responsible for protecting the interests of investors and promoting the development of the securities market. Established on April 12, 1992, under the Securities and Exchange Board of India Act, 1992, SEBI plays a crucial role in regulating and overseeing various market intermediaries, such as stock exchanges, brokers, and mutual funds. SEBI's main objective is to ensure that the securities market operates in a fair, transparent, and efficient manner.

## Key Details
SEBI is headquartered in Mumbai and has regional offices in several cities across India. The SEBI Board consists of a Chairman and several whole-time members, who are appointed by the Government of India. SEBI regulates various aspects of the securities market, including issue of capital, trading of securities, and registration of market intermediaries. The SEBI Act, 1992, and the SEBI Regulations, 1994, are the primary laws governing the functioning of SEBI. Companies and market intermediaries are required to file various forms and returns with SEBI, such as Form A, Form B, and Form C, to comply with regulatory requirements.

## Who Needs This
SEBI regulations apply to various entities, including companies listed on stock exchanges, market intermediaries such as brokers, mutual funds, and portfolio managers, and other securities market participants. Non-governmental organizations (NGOs) and companies that issue securities to raise capital or engage in securities market activities are also required to comply with SEBI regulations. Additionally, foreign companies that issue securities in India or engage in securities market activities in India are also subject to SEBI regulations.

## Step-by-Step Process
To comply with SEBI regulations, companies and market intermediaries are required to follow a step-by-step process, which includes registration with SEBI, filing of forms and returns, and compliance with regulatory requirements. For example, a company that wants to issue securities to raise capital must file a draft prospectus with SEBI, which will review the prospectus to ensure that it complies with regulatory requirements. The company must then obtain a final observation from SEBI before issuing the securities.

## Important Deadlines / Penalties
SEBI has prescribed various deadlines for filing forms and returns, and non-compliance with these deadlines can result in penalties. For example, companies are required to file their annual reports with SEBI within 60 days of the end of the financial year. Failure to file the annual report on time can result in a penalty of up to Rs. 1 lakh per day. SEBI also has the power to impose penalties on companies and market intermediaries that violate SEBI regulations, which can range from Rs. 1 lakh to Rs. 1 crore or more, depending on the nature and severity of the violation.
