# US Corporate Law
US corporate law refers to the set of rules and regulations that govern the formation, operation, and dissolution of corporations in the United States. It is a complex and multifaceted field that is shaped by federal and state laws, as well as judicial decisions and regulatory guidelines. While the Companies Act, 2013, is the primary source of Indian company law, understanding US corporate law can be important for Indian companies and NGOs that operate internationally or have dealings with US-based entities.

## Key Details
US corporate law is primarily governed by the laws of the individual states, with the majority of companies incorporated in Delaware due to its business-friendly laws and highly developed corporate law system. The key pieces of legislation that shape US corporate law include the Securities Act of 1933, the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, and the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002. Companies must file various forms with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC), such as Form 10-K and Form 10-Q, to disclose their financial performance and other material information.

## Who Needs This
US corporate law applies to any company that is incorporated in the United States, as well as foreign companies that list their securities on US exchanges or conduct significant business operations in the US. Indian NGOs and companies that have international operations or partnerships may need to understand US corporate law in order to navigate the regulatory landscape and comply with applicable laws and regulations. This may include companies that are listed on US exchanges, have US-based subsidiaries or joint ventures, or engage in cross-border transactions with US entities.

## Step-by-Step Process
The process for incorporating a company in the US typically involves the following steps: (1) choosing a business name and checking its availability, (2) filing articles of incorporation with the relevant state authorities, (3) obtaining any necessary licenses and permits, (4) issuing stock to shareholders, and (5) filing periodic reports with the SEC. Companies must also comply with ongoing regulatory requirements, such as maintaining a registered office and agent, holding annual shareholder meetings, and filing tax returns.

## Important Deadlines / Penalties
Companies that fail to comply with US corporate law may face significant penalties, including fines, lawsuits, and reputational damage. The SEC has the authority to impose civil penalties and fines on companies that violate federal securities laws, and companies may also face private lawsuits from shareholders and other stakeholders. Important deadlines include the annual filing deadline for Form 10-K, which is typically within 90 days of the company's fiscal year-end, and the quarterly filing deadline for Form 10-Q, which is typically within 45 days of the end of each fiscal quarter.
