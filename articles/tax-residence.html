Tax Residence

Tax residence refers to the status of an individual or entity being a resident of a country for tax purposes, determining which country has the right to tax their income. In India, tax residence is crucial in determining the tax liability of individuals and entities. The concept of tax residence is governed by the Income-tax Act, 1961, and is essential for understanding the tax implications for NGOs, companies, and individuals in India.

Key Details

To determine tax residence in India, an individual's physical presence in the country is considered. An individual is considered a resident if they have been in India for at least 182 days in the preceding financial year or 60 days if they have been in India for at least 365 days in the four preceding financial years. For companies, tax residence is determined by the place of incorporation or the place where the control and management of the company are situated. Form 10A is used to apply for registration of a charitable or religious trust, which is a crucial step in determining tax residence for NGOs. The threshold for tax audit is Rs 1 crore for businesses and Rs 50 lakh for professionals.

Who Needs This

Tax residence is applicable to all individuals and entities earning income in India, including NGOs, companies, and foreign nationals. NGOs registered under Section 8 of the Companies Act, 2013, or under the Societies Registration Act, 1860, need to determine their tax residence to claim exemptions under the Income-tax Act, 1961. Companies, including foreign companies with a permanent establishment in India, must also determine their tax residence to comply with tax laws. Foreign nationals working in India, including expatriates and consultants, must understand their tax residence status to avoid double taxation.

Step-by-Step Process

To determine tax residence, individuals and entities must follow these steps: 1. Calculate the number of days spent in India in the preceding financial year. 2. Determine the place of incorporation or control and management for companies. 3. File Form 10A for registration of charitable or religious trusts. 4. Obtain a PAN (Permanent Account Number) and TAN (Tax Deduction and Collection Account Number) for tax compliance. 5. File income tax returns using Form ITR-1 or Form ITR-6, depending on the type of entity.

Important Deadlines / Penalties

The due date for filing income tax returns is July 31st of each year, and the deadline for filing Form 10A is within three months from the date of creation of the trust. Failure to file returns or non-compliance with tax laws can result in penalties, including a fine of up to Rs 10,000 for late filing and up to 30% of the tax payable for non-compliance.
