Lawmaking procedure in India

The lawmaking procedure in India is a complex process that involves the Parliament of India, the state legislatures, and the executive branch of the government. It is a crucial aspect of the country's democratic framework, as it enables the creation of laws that govern the nation and its citizens. The procedure is designed to ensure that laws are made in a fair, transparent, and accountable manner.

Key Details

The lawmaking procedure in India is governed by the Constitution of India, specifically Articles 107 to 123, which outline the powers and procedures of the Parliament. The process typically begins with the introduction of a bill in either the Lok Sabha (the lower house of Parliament) or the Rajya Sabha (the upper house). The bill is then debated, discussed, and voted upon by the members of the house, and if passed, it is sent to the other house for consideration. If the bill is passed by both houses, it is sent to the President of India for assent, after which it becomes a law. The Foreign Contribution (Regulation) Act, 2010, is an example of a law that was made through this process.

Who Needs This

This lawmaking procedure applies to all individuals, companies, and organizations that are involved in the creation and implementation of laws in India. This includes members of Parliament, state legislatures, government officials, and civil society organizations, such as non-governmental organizations (NGOs). Specifically, NGOs that receive foreign contributions, such as those registered under the Foreign Contribution (Regulation) Act, 2010, need to be aware of the lawmaking procedure in India, as changes to laws and regulations can impact their operations and funding.

Step-by-Step Process

The step-by-step process of lawmaking in India involves the following stages: 1. Introduction of a bill: A bill is introduced in either the Lok Sabha or the Rajya Sabha by a minister or a member of Parliament. 2. First reading: The bill is read for the first time, and its provisions are explained to the house. 3. Referral to a committee: The bill is referred to a committee, such as a standing committee or a select committee, for examination and report. 4. Report stage: The committee presents its report to the house, and the bill is debated and discussed. 5. Voting: The bill is put to vote, and if passed, it is sent to the other house for consideration. 6. Assent: If the bill is passed by both houses, it is sent to the President of India for assent.

Important Deadlines / Penalties

There are no specific deadlines for the lawmaking procedure in India, as the process can take several months or even years to complete. However, if an NGO fails to comply with the provisions of a law, such as the Foreign Contribution (Regulation) Act, 2010, it can face penalties, including fines and cancellation of its registration.
