CSR Due Diligence

CSR due diligence is a critical process that companies undertake to evaluate the credibility and suitability of NGOs before entering into a Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) partnership. This process helps companies ensure that their CSR funds are being utilized effectively and efficiently, and that they are complying with the provisions of the Companies Act, 2013. By conducting thorough due diligence, companies can mitigate risks and build trust with their NGO partners.

Key Details

The CSR due diligence process typically involves verifying an NGO's registration, tax exemption status, and audited financial statements. Companies may also review an NGO's track record, governance structure, and compliance with relevant laws and regulations, such as the Foreign Contribution (Regulation) Act, 2010. Additionally, companies may assess an NGO's capacity to implement projects, its monitoring and evaluation systems, and its ability to provide regular progress reports.

Who Needs This

CSR due diligence applies to all companies that are required to spend a minimum of 2% of their average net profits on CSR activities, as per Section 135 of the Companies Act, 2013. This includes companies with a net worth of Rs. 500 crore or more, or a turnover of Rs. 1,000 crore or more, or a net profit of Rs. 5 crore or more. NGOs that partner with these companies must also be aware of the due diligence process and be prepared to provide the necessary information and documentation.

Step-by-Step Process

The CSR due diligence process typically involves the following steps: 1. Initial screening: Companies review an NGO's website, annual reports, and other publicly available information to assess its credibility and suitability. 2. Documentation review: Companies request and review an NGO's registration documents, tax exemption certificates, and audited financial statements. 3. Site visits: Companies may conduct site visits to assess an NGO's infrastructure, staff, and project implementation capabilities. 4. Reference checks: Companies may contact an NGO's references, such as other donors or partners, to verify its reputation and track record. 5. Final evaluation: Companies evaluate the findings from the above steps and make a decision on whether to partner with the NGO.

Important Deadlines / Penalties

Companies are required to spend their CSR funds within the financial year, and any unspent amounts must be transferred to a CSR fund or a registered trust. Failure to comply with the CSR provisions of the Companies Act, 2013, can result in penalties, including fines and imprisonment.

Related Topics

  • CSR Registration: Companies must register their CSR initiatives on the MCA21 portal.
  • NGO Registration: NGOs must register under the relevant laws, such as the Societies Registration Act, 1860, or the Trusts Act, 1882.
  • CSR Reporting: Companies must file their CSR reports with the Ministry of Corporate Affairs using Form CSR-2.
  • FCRA Registration: NGOs that receive foreign funding must register under the Foreign Contribution (Regulation) Act, 2010.
  • Tax Exemption: NGOs must obtain tax exemption certificates, such as 12A and 80G certificates, to avail of tax benefits.
