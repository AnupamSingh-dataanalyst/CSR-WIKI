the Income-tax Act, 1961

The Income-tax Act, 1961 is a comprehensive legislation that governs the taxation of income in India, empowering the central government to tax non-agricultural income. This act, along with the Income Tax Rules 1962, Notifications, and Circulars issued by the Central Board of Direct Taxes (CBDT), forms the backbone of income tax law in India. The act has undergone numerous amendments over the years, with the most recent changes aimed at simplifying tax compliance and promoting transparency.

Key Details

The Income-tax Act, 1961 consists of 23 chapters and 298 sections, covering various aspects of income taxation, including residential status, income computation, tax deductions, and exemptions. Key concepts include the distinction between agricultural and non-agricultural income, with agricultural income defined in Section 10(1) of the act. Taxpayers are required to file their income tax returns using forms such as ITR-1, ITR-2, ITR-3, and ITR-4, depending on their income sources and slab.

Who Needs This

The Income-tax Act, 1961 applies to all individuals, Hindu Undivided Families (HUFs), companies, firms, and other entities that earn income in India. Non-Governmental Organizations (NGOs) and companies registered under the Companies Act, 2013, are also required to comply with the provisions of the act. Specifically, NGOs that receive foreign contributions must comply with the Foreign Contribution (Regulation) Act, 2010, and file their tax returns using Form FC-3.

Step-by-Step Process

To comply with the Income-tax Act, 1961, taxpayers must follow a step-by-step process: (1) determine their residential status, (2) compute their taxable income, (3) claim deductions and exemptions, (4) file their income tax return using the relevant form, and (5) pay any tax due or claim a refund. NGOs and companies must also maintain accurate financial records and obtain a Permanent Account Number (PAN) from the Income Tax Department.

Important Deadlines / Penalties

The income tax return filing deadline for individuals is typically July 31st of each year, while companies and NGOs must file their returns by September 30th. Failure to file tax returns or pay tax due can result in penalties, including a late filing fee of up to Rs. 10,000 and interest on the outstanding tax amount. In severe cases, taxpayers may also face prosecution under the act.
