# Tolerance tax
Tolerance tax refers to the permissible limit of variation between the actual and declared value of goods or services, beyond which a penalty or fine is imposed. In the context of income tax in India, tolerance tax is relevant when there are discrepancies in the valuation of assets, income, or expenses. The concept of tolerance tax is crucial in ensuring compliance with tax laws and avoiding unnecessary litigation.

## Key Details
The Income-tax Act, 1961, and the Income Tax Rules, 1962, govern the provisions related to tolerance tax in India. The Central Board of Direct Taxes (CBDT) issues notifications and circulars that provide guidance on the permissible limits of variation. For instance, in the case of valuations, a variation of up to 10% to 15% may be considered tolerable, depending on the specific circumstances.

## Who Needs This
Tolerance tax is relevant to all taxpayers, including individuals, companies, and non-governmental organizations (NGOs), that are required to file income tax returns in India. NGOs, in particular, need to be aware of the tolerance tax provisions to ensure that their financial statements and tax returns are accurate and compliant with the law. Companies and businesses that engage in transactions involving valuation of assets, such as property or shares, also need to consider the tolerance tax provisions.

## Step-by-Step Process
While there is no specific step-by-step process for tolerance tax, taxpayers can follow these general steps to ensure compliance:
* Maintain accurate financial records and accounts
* Ensure that valuations are based on credible and reliable methods
* Disclose all relevant information in the income tax return
* Seek professional advice from a chartered accountant or tax consultant, if necessary

## Important Deadlines / Penalties
The deadlines for filing income tax returns in India are typically July 31st for individuals and October 31st for companies and NGOs that require audit. Failure to comply with the tolerance tax provisions can result in penalties, fines, and even prosecution. The penalties can range from 100% to 300% of the tax amount, depending on the severity of the offense.
