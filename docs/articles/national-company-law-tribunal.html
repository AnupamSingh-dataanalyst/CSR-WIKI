National Company Law Tribunal

The National Company Law Tribunal (NCLT) is a quasi-judicial body in India that adjudicates issues related to Indian companies. Established under the Companies Act, 2013, it replaced the Company Law Board and aims to provide a faster and more efficient dispute resolution mechanism for corporate entities. The NCLT has jurisdiction over a wide range of matters, including company mergers, acquisitions, and winding-up proceedings.

Key Details

The NCLT has 16 benches across India, with its principal bench located in New Delhi. It is headed by a President, who is a retired High Court Judge, and consists of Judicial and Technical Members. The NCLT has the power to hear and dispose of cases under the Companies Act, 2013, the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016, and other related laws. Key forms used in NCLT proceedings include Form NCLT-1 for filing petitions and Form NCLT-3 for filing appeals.

Who Needs This

The NCLT is relevant to all companies registered under the Companies Act, 2013, including public and private limited companies, as well as limited liability partnerships (LLPs). Non-governmental organizations (NGOs) that are registered as companies or societies may also need to interact with the NCLT in case of disputes or other corporate matters. Additionally, foreign companies with a presence in India may need to approach the NCLT for certain matters, such as mergers and acquisitions or winding-up proceedings.

Step-by-Step Process

To file a petition with the NCLT, the following steps can be followed: 1. Determine the relevant bench of the NCLT that has jurisdiction over the matter. 2. Prepare and file the petition in Form NCLT-1, along with the required fee and supporting documents. 3. Serve a copy of the petition on the respondent(s). 4. Appear before the NCLT on the scheduled hearing date and argue the case. 5. Receive the order or judgment of the NCLT and comply with its directions.

Important Deadlines / Penalties

The NCLT has strict deadlines for filing petitions and appeals, and non-compliance can result in penalties, including dismissal of the petition or imposition of costs. For example, an appeal against an order of the NCLT must be filed within 45 days of the order, and a delay beyond this period can result in the appeal being dismissed.
