# Non-tax revenue
Non-tax revenue refers to the income generated by the government from sources other than taxes, such as fees, fines, and dividends from public sector enterprises. In the context of Indian NGOs and companies, understanding non-tax revenue is crucial for compliance with various regulations and laws. The concept of non-tax revenue is closely related to the Income-tax Act, 1961, and the Seventh Schedule to the Constitution of India.

## Key Details
Non-tax revenue in India includes income from various sources, such as interest on loans, dividends, and profits from public sector enterprises. The Central Board of Direct Taxes (CBDT) is responsible for administering the income-tax law, which consists of the Income-tax Act, 1961, Income Tax Rules 1962, Notifications, and Circulars. The Finance Acts, which are annual budgetary documents, also play a crucial role in determining non-tax revenue. For instance, the Finance Act, 2022, introduced new provisions for taxing digital assets, which are considered non-tax revenue.

## Who Needs This
Non-tax revenue applies to all NGOs and companies in India that generate income from sources other than taxes. This includes public sector enterprises, private companies, and non-profit organizations that receive grants, donations, or interest on investments. Specifically, NGOs registered under the Income-tax Act, 1961, such as those with 12A and 80G certificates, need to understand non-tax revenue to ensure compliance with tax laws and regulations.

## Step-by-Step Process
To comply with non-tax revenue regulations, NGOs and companies should follow these steps:
1. Identify all sources of non-tax revenue, such as interest on loans, dividends, and grants.
2. Maintain accurate records of all non-tax revenue transactions.
3. File annual returns with the CBDT, using forms such as the ITR-6 and ITR-7.
4. Ensure compliance with the Income-tax Act, 1961, and other relevant laws and regulations.

## Important Deadlines / Penalties
NGOs and companies must file their annual returns by the prescribed deadlines, which are typically July 31st or October 31st of each year, depending on the type of return. Failure to file returns or comply with non-tax revenue regulations can result in penalties, fines, and even prosecution. For instance, the CBDT can impose a penalty of up to Rs. 1 lakh for failure to file an annual return.
