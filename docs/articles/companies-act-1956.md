# Companies Act, 1956
The Companies Act, 1956 was a landmark legislation in India that governed the incorporation, management, and operation of companies. It was a comprehensive law that regulated various aspects of companies, including their formation, financing, and winding up. The Act was repealed by the Companies Act, 2013, but its provisions continue to have an impact on the corporate sector in India.

## Key Details
The Companies Act, 1956 consisted of 658 sections, 15 schedules, and 22 parts, making it one of the most extensive pieces of legislation in India at the time. The Act applied to all types of companies, including private and public companies, limited by shares or guarantee, and unlimited companies. The minimum paid-up capital required for a private company was Rs. 1 lakh, while for a public company it was Rs. 5 lakhs. The Act also introduced various forms, such as Form 1, Form 5, and Form 32, which were used for registration, annual returns, and other purposes.

## Who Needs This
The Companies Act, 1956 applied to all companies incorporated in India, including NGOs and non-profit companies registered under Section 25 of the Act. This meant that NGOs and non-profit organizations that were registered as companies had to comply with the provisions of the Act, including filing annual returns and maintaining accounts. Companies with a minimum paid-up capital of Rs. 5 lakhs or more were required to have their accounts audited by a chartered accountant.

## Step-by-Step Process
Although the Companies Act, 1956 has been repealed, its provisions continue to apply to companies that were incorporated under the Act. For companies that were registered under the Act, the step-by-step process for compliance included:
* Filing Form 1 for registration
* Filing Form 5 for annual returns
* Maintaining accounts and getting them audited
* Filing Form 32 for changes in directors or managers
* Holding annual general meetings and filing returns with the Registrar of Companies

## Important Deadlines / Penalties
The Companies Act, 1956 imposed various penalties for non-compliance, including fines and imprisonment. The deadlines for filing annual returns and other forms were strict, and companies that failed to comply were liable to be penalized. The Act also provided for the winding up of companies that failed to comply with its provisions.
