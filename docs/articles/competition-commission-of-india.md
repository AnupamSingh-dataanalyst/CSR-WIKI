# Competition Commission of India
The Competition Commission of India (CCI) is a statutory body established under the Competition Act, 2002, to promote competition and prevent anti-competitive practices in India. The CCI is responsible for enforcing the provisions of the Competition Act, which aims to protect the interests of consumers and promote economic efficiency. The CCI operates under the administrative control of the Ministry of Corporate Affairs.

## Key Details
The CCI consists of a Chairperson and up to six members, appointed by the Central Government. The Commission has the power to investigate allegations of anti-competitive agreements, abuse of dominant position, and combinations (mergers and acquisitions) that may have an adverse effect on competition. The CCI also has the power to impose penalties on companies found guilty of violating the Competition Act, up to 10% of the company's average turnover for the last three years.

## Who Needs This
The CCI's regulations apply to all businesses operating in India, including companies, partnerships, and sole proprietorships. NGOs and companies involved in mergers and acquisitions, joint ventures, or other combinations must notify the CCI if the combined turnover of the parties exceeds Rs. 3000 crore in India or Rs. 9000 crore globally. Additionally, companies with a dominant position in the market must be cautious not to abuse their position, as this can attract penalties from the CCI.

## Step-by-Step Process
To notify the CCI of a combination, the parties must submit a Form I or Form II, depending on the complexity of the transaction. The notification must be made within 30 days of the triggering event, such as the execution of a binding agreement or the approval of a merger by the boards of the companies involved. The CCI will then review the notification and may request additional information or conduct an investigation to determine whether the combination is likely to have an adverse effect on competition.

## Important Deadlines / Penalties
The CCI has the power to impose penalties on companies that fail to notify a combination or provide false or misleading information. The penalty for failure to notify a combination can be up to 1% of the company's average turnover for the last three years. Additionally, companies that are found to have engaged in anti-competitive practices may face penalties of up to 10% of their average turnover for the last three years.
